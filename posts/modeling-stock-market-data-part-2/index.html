Modeling Stock Market Data - Part 2

Posted Dec 13, 2015 2015-12-13T07:00:00+02:00 by S. Aya

After downloading the market data and deal with the cleaning process in the first part; it is time to move on to exploration phase to gain insight on data. In order to accomplish this task, one should have an end goal in mind. Random graphs may take you a step further, but real value in data emerges from the careful explorations that can only be gained with experience in the field of study. I don’t say I’m an expert in this field, but I know that there are times that only a sentence in a book makes that book important among other finance/trading books. This blog post won’t get you richer nor master in anything related to trading, but the analysis steps we take are important and minor changes in later parts (for stock selection rules) would give you great results. To sum up, this post addresses the above challeges, with major emphasis on the data analysis.

For the last part of the analysis I will create a dataframe finA by merging finM and pvtab on Sector column. Therefore we’ll have “Price/Sales”, “Price/Book”, “Enterprise Value/Revenue”, “Enterprise Value/EBITDA”, “Qtrly Revenue Growth” and the mean values for pertinent sector next to them. Next, we’ll create zero columns for scoring that will be explained shortly.

finA = pd.merge(finM[['Sector', 'Code', 'Price/Book', 'Price/Sales', "Enterprise Value/Revenue", "Enterprise Value/EBITDA", "Qtrly Revenue Growth"]], pvtab, on='Sector')

finA['P/B Score']= 0; finA['P/S Score'] = 0; finA['EV/R Score'] = 0;
finA['EV/EBITDA Score'] = 0; finA['Qtrly Rev Score'] = 0;

With the comparison of the values to the average values we’ll create scores and eventually a relative index value (named as Overall Score) at the end.

1
2
3
4
5

finA.loc[pd.to_numeric(finA['Price/Book']) > finA['Price/Book AVG'], 'P/B Score'] = 1
finA.loc[pd.to_numeric(finA['Price/Sales'], errors='coerce') > finA['Price/Sales AVG'], 'P/S Score'] = 1
finA.loc[pd.to_numeric(finA['Enterprise Value/Revenue'], errors='coerce') > finA['Enterprise Value/Revenue AVG'], 'EV/R Score'] = 1
finA.loc[pd.to_numeric(finA['Enterprise Value/EBITDA'], errors='coerce') > finA['Enterprise Value/EBITDA AVG'], 'EV/EBITDA Score'] = 1
finA.loc[pd.to_numeric(finA['Qtrly Revenue Growth'], errors='coerce') > finA['Qtrly Revenue Growth AVG'], 'Qtrly Rev Score'] = 1

Summing up the scores that obtained by comparison of the Sector averages to the stock’s values in each row we will obtain `Overall Score, a relative index value that will help with the selecting the good stocks.

2

finA['Overall Score'] = finA[['P/B Score', 'P/S Score', 'EV/R Score', 'EV/EBITDA Score', 'Qtrly Rev Score']].sum(axis=1)
finA = finA.sort_values(by='Overall Score', ascending=False)

Summary

This post and the previous one is about Modeling of the Stock Market Data, with major emphasis on the data analysis. We used Turkish BIST Market Data from Yahoo Finance page using scraping tools. Also company names and sectors are retreived from the Public Disclosure Platform. Getting and cleaning the data was the objective of the first part. In this post, using the clean data from the previous part, we created a basic rule: Compare sectoral average of the selected statistics to individual stocks. This comparison resulted with relative index values for each stock. Sorting the results gave us the top choices according to our criteria. A quick look to the histroical price graphics of these top stocks shows that the results are good. Thus the technical analysis part comes next, but before that we have some other analyses next.
